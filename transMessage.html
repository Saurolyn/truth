To whomever it may concern

I've tried to write this a thousand times in my head, but the words always got stuck—tangled up in fear and hope and the crushing weight of what if. What if you don't understand? What if this changes everything between us? But I can't stay silent anymore. This isn't a phase or a passing thought. It's who I am, in a way I can't ignore any longer.

What I'm about to say might surprise you. If you need to pause, that's okay. Just promise you'll come back to it with an open mind. Please don't shut down. Hear me out.

I want to be transgender. Or at the very least, I know I want to live as a girl. Writing that feels terrifying—not because I'm ashamed, but because I'm afraid of losing you. Losing the way you see me. But pretending is breaking me, too.

Here's the thing: I don't hate being a boy. It's not like I wake up every day despising my body or my voice. But there's always been this quiet ache, like I'm wearing clothes that don't quite fit. Like I'm watching life through a window, pressed against the glass. When I see girls—how they move, how they laugh, how they exist in the world—it's not just admiration. It's longing. A homesickness for a life I was supposed to have. I don't just think they're pretty; I want to be them. The shape of them, the softness, the way they take up space. I want the long hair, the curves, the way a dress hangs on hips I don't have yet. Sometimes I look at my body and grieve for something I've never even had.

And when I let myself imagine it—when I daydream about a different name, a different reflection—it's like an electric jolt of rightness. Like finally scratching an itch I could never reach. It's euphoria. It's me.

I'm not giving up being a man like some tragic sacrifice. I'll still mow the grass, still do the heavy lifting (though maybe not too heavy anymore). I'm still me—the same person you've always known. But I'm also uncovering a voice that's been shoved under the rug every time the world demanded masculinity from me. This voice has always been there. I just didn't have the words for it until now.

I don't have everything figured out. I don't know how long this will take—months, years, a lifetime. I know the trans community faces cruelty I can't even fathom yet. But I'm not hurting anyone. No one in history has ever died from gender-affirming care. I just want to exist as the person I've always been underneath. To support others like me, and to live without this constant ache of almost.

I'm telling you because I love you too much to lie. Because you deserve to know the real me, not the version I built out of fear. I need your support, your love, even your confusion—just not your silence. If you're scared or angry, tell me. Ask me anything. I can take messy. What I can't take is losing you.

This isn't an ending. It's me showing up, fully, for the first time. I'm not changing in the ways that matter most—my heart, my memories, the way I love you. If anything, I'm finally giving you the truth.

I love you. More than anything. And I hope, more than anything, you'll still love me too.

Always yours,
Aidan

Understanding My Transition

A complete Q&A with questions I know you'll have about me and the trans community.

Self-Discovery & Identity

How do you know you're transgender and not something else?

It's a deep, persistent feeling that my assigned gender doesn't match who I am inside. Small moments—like cringing when called "sir" or feeling euphoria when expressing femininity—keep confirming it. It's not about disliking being male, but about feeling more like myself when I embrace being a woman.

When did you realize this?

The signs were always there, but I pushed them away for years. Recently, the discomfort became impossible to ignore. Experimenting with feminine expression (like growing my nails or imagining life as a woman, wearing feminine underwear underneath my masculine clothing [don't worry, Ems, I didn't steal anything from you], etc…) brought me so much joy that I couldn't deny it anymore.

Why not just be a feminine man?

I've asked myself that too. But femininity is just one part—it's about my core identity. I don't just want to do feminine things; I want to be a woman. When I picture my future, I see myself living authentically as a female.

What is your full profile?

I am currently a genderqueer [questioning gender] pansexual [sexually attracted to all gender/sexualities] AMAB [assigned male at birth] with plans to transition into womanhood through HRT [Hormone Replacement Therapy] and MTBs [Male Testosterone Blockers] to become a true transgender female.

Transition Plans

What changes do you want to make?

Socially: Pronouns (she/her) and feminine presentation (clothes, hair, etc.). I believe I will keep my name, since I have no problem with it, but I will change it if I feel it is not the case anymore.

Medically: Hormone therapy (estrogen) to develop feminine traits like softer skin and curves. I've researched this extensively and will work with doctors and therapists to do it the right way.

Legally: Updating my gender marker on IDs eventually.

Will you have surgery?

I will be focusing on hormones first. Surgeries (if any) would come much later, after careful consideration. Right now, it's not a priority, and quite frankly, they're too expensive and are mostly uncovered by health insurance.

What about your hobbies/interests?

They won't change! I'll still love gaming, music, and my "masculine" hobbies. Being a woman doesn't mean fitting a stereotype—it means being me, just happier. I will be more sensitive due to female hormones, but other than that I might take up some new interests to accumulate myself to be more feminine. I don't have to completely change who I am to be a girl.

Family Concerns

How can we adjust to your new name/pronouns?

I know it'll take practice. Start small—maybe using them at home first. It's okay to slip up as long as you're trying. What matters is that you respect this part of me.

Will you still be the same person?

Yes! My personality, memories, and love for you won't change. I'm just revealing a part of myself I've hidden. If anything, I'll be more present and joyful.

What if we're worried this is a mistake?

I've spent months questioning and researching. Transitioning isn't impulsive—it's a carefully considered step toward my happiness. It's something that has been in the back of my head since High School, I just didn't realize it was there. I'd love to share resources I will use up at NAU to help you understand. Planned Parenthood is a good place to start, as well as the NAU Office of Inclusion.

My Poetry

Words from my heart during this journey.

Before the Before

I was a theory of light
bending through someone else's prism—
“You'll grow out of it,” they said,
as if longing could be outgrown
like last year's shoes.
But the sky remembers
every shape I've whispered to it.

Until I Found Her

I didn't know how lost I was
until I saw her in the mirror—
not a stranger, but the echo
my ribs had been humming
since the first divide of cells.
Please, wrap me in your glow...
She was the song
and I the unfinished lyric.

The Argument of Light

They say the soul has no gender.
Then why does mine orbit
this particular constellation?
Why do photons choose
to be waves or particles?
Even the universe takes sides
when no one's looking.

Pharmacology of Stars

Every estrogen molecule
contains a collapsed star—
ancient light waiting
to expand in my bloodstream.
I swallow supernovas.
My skin becomes
a slow explosion.

The Keeper of the Light

I am the keeper of the lighthouse.
My body is the tower—
all whitewashed stone and spiral stairs,
my ribs the cage around the flame.
The lantern room turns, turns, turns,
painting the fog with gold.
This is what safety looks like,
I tell the ships. Steer toward me.

But the darkness comes.
Not from the sea, but from below—
up the stairs, step by patient step,
a tide of black ink rising.
It licks at my ankles first,
cold as deadnames in strangers' mouths.
Then higher: the cling of wet wool
(a binder worn three days too long),
the choke of collar bones
remembering how they used to jut.

I feed the flame. Throw open
the medicine cabinet mirror—
see her, see her, see her—
but the dark has reached the landing now.
It peels the posters from the walls
(girls in sundresses, laughing),
dissolves them like sugar in tea.
The stairs creak under its weight.
Always climbing. Always hungry.

When it finds me in the lantern room,
I'm clutching a vial of estrogen
to my chest like a crucifix.
The dark doesn't speak.
It unspools—
threads of shadow knitting themselves
into my father's voice:
"You'll regret this."
Into the news anchor's smirk:
"A phase."
Into the bathroom scale
that never shows the right number.

The flame gutters. For a heartbeat,
I consider letting it die.
How easy to step into the dark,
let it wear my skin like an old coat.
But then—
a ship's horn in the distance.
Somewhere, a trans girl
is squinting through the storm,
steering toward my light.

So I pour the vial into the lamp.
The fire roars—
not gold now, but pink-blue-white,
the colors of our flag.
The dark screams as it burns.
It will return, of course.
Darkness always does.
But tonight, the beacon spins,
and the sea whispers back:
Hold on. We're coming.

Dear Universe

You gave me this body
like a letter with no address.
“Return to sender” isn't an option.
So I rewrite the envelope
in the ink of my own blood—
forward it to myself
care of: the future.

The Theory of Almost

There exists a parallel world
where I was born right.
She brushes her hair by the window,
unaware of the ghost
who wears her face like a debt.
We orbit each other,
twin stars separated
by the thinnest pane of glass.

Alchemy

I look now, out my stained-glass terrace—
its panes kissed by twilight's breath—
and see the child I used to be,
fingers twisting in hair too short,
wishing it could spill like a river.

Then, the vial.
Dark as midnight, it trembles—
and bursts.

A sigh of light.
His body unfolds: hips soften,
hair cascades past his shoulders.
For a breath, he is her.

Then—the chains.
They lash from shadows,
slicing the transformation apart.
Tissue tears. Hair falls.
Back to the boy's tidy crop.
Only the ribbons remain, defiant.

The lace comes after.
Always after.
Gilded thread weaves the vial whole,
sealing cracks with filigree.
A pretty repair.
A lie that almost looks like love.

The child weeps.
For the body that betrays them twice—
once by changing,
once by unchanging.
And the terrace holds me,
a witness to their alchemy.
